# predictive coding (neuroscience)

## 導讀

大腦不是直接感知世界(direct, unfiltered access to reality is a misconception)，而是基於過往經驗不斷構建內部模型來預測未來，並通過比對預測與實際的偏差來學習、更新認知，而感知失衡則可能導致精神疾病。預測模型過於強勢易導致精神分裂（schizophrenia; override reality），過於弱勢易導致自閉症（autism）. 

## 正文

What if I told you that you are already an expert at predicting the future? You do it all the time, right now—though you might look skeptical. Well, what's going to happen next? It seems obvious. This might feel trivial because we do it effortlessly, but it's not. To know that water will spill when a bucket tips, you need an understanding of gravity, fluid dynamics, and cause and effect. In a split second, your brain integrates all this knowledge to anticipate what happens next. You can almost feel the cold shock of water splashing over your head and running down your neck. We perform this kind of prediction constantly, with ease.

You can even predict the last word of this sentence. You hear about something happening to someone else, and you intuitively know what it would feel like if it happened to you. This is the basis of empathy. **We are continuously running a simulation—an internal model of the world—in our heads to forecast what will happen next. This is what we call thinking. It allows us to imagine the consequences of an action without ever having to perform it.**

We know this is true, partly thanks to phenomena like visual illusions, but it happens far beyond these parlor tricks. **The idea that we have direct, unfiltered access to reality is a misconception.** So, why do we do this? Why constantly predict? The advantages are clear: we can anticipate events and, crucially, we can filter the overwhelming flood of sensory information, attending only to what deviates from our expectations. Anticipating the trajectory of an oncoming car is what lets you avoid a collision. Filtering out the unchanging details of the stage you've been staring at is what allows you to focus on my voice.

But how does this actually work in the brain? To answer this, we turn to neuroscience—and these are precisely the questions my lab is investigating: How does the brain generate predictions, and what are they used for?

To find out, we train mice to play a video game. The mouse sits on a spherical treadmill, and its locomotion controls its movement through a virtual environment displayed on a sort of "IMAX" screen for mice. As the mouse plays, we record the activity of neurons in its brain, specifically in the primary visual cortex, the area responsible for processing visual information. We use advanced imaging techniques to watch the activity of individual brain cells in real-time. In essence, we can watch the mouse think.

Now, what happens as the mouse first explores a virtual environment and learns its rules? Imagine a mouse learns to run down a corridor. At the end, there's a turn, and behind that turn is a specific image. The first few times, the mouse turns, sees the image, and this triggers a strong visual response in its brain. But what happens after it has repeated this journey hundreds of times? Mice, as it turns out, quite enjoy this.

By the two-hundredth time, the mouse has learned exactly what to expect. As it approaches the familiar corner, its brain generates a powerful prediction of the image it is about to see. This prediction is so strong that it begins to suppress the actual visual response to the image itself. The brain is effectively saying, "I know what's there; I don't need to process it fully."

Now, what if we violate that prediction? The mouse, expecting the image, rounds the corner, but the image is gone. We have removed it. In that moment, the mouse's brain erupts with a massive signal—a strong prediction error. This is the signature of surprise, the moment the brain realizes its internal model is wrong. And this is precisely the moment when learning happens, when the internal model of the world is updated.

We are constantly comparing what we expect to see with what we actually see. We predict the mundane so that we can instantly detect salient deviations from those predictions. These deviations—these prediction errors—are what trigger us to update our understanding of the world.

Consider learning to speak. We compare an internal model of the word we intend to say with the sound of our own voice as we say it. This is precisely why your voice sounds so different on a recording; what you hear when you speak is a blend of what you expect to hear (your internal model) and what you actually hear. These predictions can be so powerful that they override reality, causing us to miss glaring typos in our own writing, or to not even notice a partner's new haircut or our own new glasses. We see what we expected to see.

Perception, therefore, is always a comparison—a dynamic negotiation between what we predict we will hear, see, feel, and smell, and what is actually there.

Why does this matter? Imagine, for a moment, that this finely tuned balance between prediction and sensory input were to become skewed.

If the balance tipped too far in favor of predictions, one might begin to see or hear things that aren't there, or project intentions onto others that don't exist. This is the world of hallucinations and delusions. Conversely, if predictions were too weak, the world would appear perpetually unpredictable and even magical. The intentions of others would be a bewildering mystery. One might resort to rigid, repetitive behaviors in a desperate attempt to impose predictability on a chaotic world.

The former are hallmarks of what we call schizophrenia; the latter, autism. From this perspective, these neurological conditions can be viewed not as categorical breaks from normality, but as extremes resulting from an imbalance in the very same predictive machinery we all use every day.

So, what does this mean for us, here today? **The next time you find yourself in profound disagreement with someone, consider this: our expectations are built from our own deeply personal history of interactions with the world. We differ in how we perceive reality in far more fundamental ways** than simply how we judge the color of a dress. **We each construct our own simulation—our own unique reality—as the very foundation of learning and navigating the future.** We don't just see the world; we are constantly, actively predicting it into being.
